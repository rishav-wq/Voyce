## 1. Core belief

**Your pipeline dies from silence, not from bad writing — so the thing worth automating is showing up, not drafting.**

## 2. The narrative

*(customer-facing, ~460 words)*

**You didn't go quiet because you ran out of things to say.**

You're good at the work — that was never the question. But if you're a consultant or fractional executive, LinkedIn is your storefront: clients follow your posts for months before they ever reach out, so visibility today is the pipeline you close next quarter. And the old game gave you two ways to stay visible: grind out every post yourself between client calls, or pay a ghostwriter $1,000–$5,000 a month to do it for you.

Most people chose a third option without meaning to: post in a burst when things were slow, go silent the moment client work picked up. The cruel part is the lag — the invoice for silence arrives a quarter late. One day you look up and it's: *"I usually have work booked out two quarters ahead. Right now? I'm not even full for this quarter."* A dry pipeline this quarter is a decision you made three months ago, one skipped post at a time.

**Then AI showed up — and made it worse.** A wave of tools promised to fix this. What they actually shipped was a draft queue. You still open the app every day. You still spend 15–30 minutes sanding the robot out of each post. You still hit publish yourself. They sped up the writing and left you holding the habit — and the habit was the problem all along. ChatGPT is the same trap with fewer buttons: prompt, edit, copy, paste, daily, until you stop. Meanwhile the feed filled with generic AI text, readers learned to spot it instantly, and LinkedIn started burying it. "More content, faster" stopped being a strategy and became a liability.

**The new game** isn't generating drafts faster. It's showing up consistently — your take, in your voice — without babysitting anything. Not a queue you stock. Not a retainer you manage. Delegation, with a kill switch.

That's Voyce. Three minutes of setup, then it runs the whole loop: finds today's news in your niche, writes your take in your voice — learned from your actual posts, aimed at the bar readers actually reward: one real datapoint, one honest opinion, one scar from doing the work — and publishes on schedule while you're with clients. Every post logged with the source it reacted to. Preview everything before you arm it. Pause with one click. Official LinkedIn API — the same door Buffer walks through.

To be clear about who this is for: if you enjoy writing and want a better editor, keep your copilot — it's good at that. Voyce is for the expert whose calendar belongs to clients.

Schedulers post what you queued. Copilots draft what you'll edit. Voyce shows up so you don't have to — $49/month, the ghostwriter outcome without the ghostwriter invoice.

**You stay the expert. You stay with your clients. You just never go invisible again.**

## 4. Messaging hierarchy

### Primary message

**"The AI ghostwriter that actually posts."**
Every other tool hands you a draft. Voyce finds the news, writes your take in your voice, and hits publish — while you're with clients.
*(This executes the ICP's decided primary positioning verbatim — existing searched category + the one-column differentiator, price-anchored to the human service.)*

### Pillar 1 — It replaces the habit, not just the writing

*The claim:* Voyce eliminates the daily decision to post. No queue to stock, no draft to babysit, no app to open.

*Proof point:* It is the only software that closes the full loop on **today's** news unattended — same-day niche news → voice-matched post → published, zero keystrokes. Taplio, Supergrow, ContentIn, and Scripe all stop at a draft you still edit and schedule (users report 15–30 minutes of editing per AI draft); ViralBrain auto-publishes only a pre-approved 30-day evergreen queue and structurally cannot react to yesterday's news. Nobody else even claims habit elimination — they all sell faster writing.

### Pillar 2 — The ghostwriter outcome, not the ghostwriter invoice

*The claim:* The only other thing that removes you from the loop entirely is a human — and the human costs a client's worth of fees and still demands your interview time every week.

*Proof point:* Human LinkedIn ghostwriters run $1,000–$5,000/month (median $2–5K, Foundera 2026). Voyce is $49/month with a one-time 3-minute setup — roughly 1/70th of the median.

*(Discipline rule, imported from ICP §3 claim 5: **price is the kicker, never the pillar on its own.** The anchor is always the human ghostwriter, never the $19 tools — Supergrow is cheaper and the auto-publish edge is one feature flag deep for competitors. Never lead with price alone.)*

### Pillar 3 — Built to be trusted with your name

*The claim:* An autopilot flying your professional reputation had better show its work — so it does. Voyce is delegation with a kill switch, not a black box.

*Proof point:* Every post is logged with the news source it reacted to; preview/dry-run mode shows exactly what it would post before you arm it; one click pauses a profile; publishing goes through LinkedIn's official OAuth API — the same mechanism Buffer and Hootsuite use, no scraping, no cookies. No competitor markets an audit trail for autonomous posting — and since "what if it posts something wrong under my name?" is the category's #1 objection, this is the conversion mechanism, not a footnote.

### Kicker (comparison pages, narrative closers — not a pillar)

> Schedulers post what you queued. Copilots draft what you'll edit. Voyce shows up so you don't have to.

## 5. Words we use / words we ban

### Words we use

| Word / phrase | Why |
|---|---|
| **"It hits publish" / "it posts"** | The one-column differentiator; instantly graspable |
| **"Sounds like me" / "in your voice, your take"** | The exact phrase the ICP uses for the desired outcome (§5) — own it |
| **"While you're with clients"** | Frames absence as billable work, not neglect |
| **"It's handled"** | The delegator psychology of the fractional-exec beachhead |
| **"Kill switch" / "pause anytime" / "preview first"** | Answers the #1 objection before it's asked |
| **"Logged, with the source"** | Provenance is the conversion mechanism, not a footnote |
| **"Official LinkedIn API — the same door Buffer uses"** | The tested counter to ban fear, verbatim from ICP §5 |
| **"Pipeline" / "client conversations"** | The outcome metric we want to own (never impressions) |
| **"Showing up" / "the habit" / "staying visible" / "going quiet"** | Names the real competitor: habit failure |
| **"Blank composer" / "burst-posting" / "draft-queue babysitting"** | Names the enemy in concrete, felt terms |
| **"One real datapoint, one honest opinion, one scar from doing the work"** | The customers' own quality bar — the bar Voyce posts aim at |
| **"Ghostwriter outcome, without the ghostwriter invoice"** | Anchors price to $1–5K/mo humans, not $19 tools |
| **"Today's news"** | vs evergreen filler — the structural claim ViralBrain can't make |

### Words we ban

| Banned | Why |
|---|---|
| **"Zero-touch" / "no human in the loop" / "set and forget"** | Full-autonomy headlines trigger churn and distrust in brand-sensitive contexts (ICP verdict); sell delegation with a kill switch instead |
| **"Autopilot" as a standalone headline** | Polluted by spam-adjacent tools (~40% of accounts on non-compliant automation restricted Jan–Mar 2026); fine in-product or mid-body when paired with control language, never as the naked promise |
| **"LinkedIn automation" / "automate your LinkedIn"** | The spam-adjacent, ban-risk category we position *against* |
| **"Content at scale" / "10x your content" / "never run out of content"** | Volume framing = slop framing; the algorithm and the readers both punish it |
| **"AI-generated content"** | Readers who spot AI think "this person couldn't be bothered"; we sell *your take* — AI is the mechanism, not the product |
| **"Viral" / "impressions" / "followers" / "engagement hacks" / "auto-DM" / "auto-connect" / "pods"** | Wrong outcome metric, and the engagement-automation category is the ban-risk we position against |
| **"Cheapest" / "cheaper than X"** | Supergrow is cheaper; price positioning only works anchored to the human ghostwriter |
| **"Game-changing" / "revolutionize" / "unleash" / "supercharge" / "in today's fast-paced world"** | The exact vocabulary of the AI slop we claim to be against; slop tells in our own copy kill the product claim on contact |
| **"Daily" as a hard promise in headlines** | Open question in the ICP (§6.2 — best proof-point consultant posts 2x/week); say "on schedule" / "consistently" until cadence data is in |
| **Any stat or testimonial we can't cite** | The audit trail is the brand — provenance IS the product; fabrication anywhere kills it everywhere. Everything in this doc traces to `01-icp.md`; keep it that way |

*Original decision list preserved below for the record.*

1. **[FOUNDER — headline promise] Zero-touch vs one-tap approval.** This draft sells "delegation with a kill switch" per the ICP verdict (§1, §6.1) and never promises "no approval, ever." If your first validation interviews demand the approval tap as the default, Pillar 3 becomes the primary message and the approval queue must ship before launch copy hardens. Decide the default posture now, because it shapes every headline.

2. **[FOUNDER — promise near the product edge] The quality-bar claim.** The narrative says Voyce posts "aim at" the bar of "one real datapoint, one honest opinion, one scar from doing the work." ICP §6.3 flags whether unattended news-reaction posts can actually clear that bar as *the existential quality question*. "Aim at" is hedged on purpose — ratify that hedge, or cut the sentence until the 14-post reach test (autopilot vs manual baseline) is run.

3. **[FOUNDER — cadence language] "On schedule / consistently" vs "every day."** The product does post daily (PITCH.md), but ICP §6.2 says daily may scare this ICP (best proof-point consultant posts 2x/week). This draft uses "on schedule" throughout. Ratify the hedge, or overrule and promise "daily."

4. **[FOUNDER — pricing, blocking outreach] $29 vs ₹2,499 vs ₹1,499–1,699.** Narrative and value prop use $29/mo (global). ICP §6.4 says the live site shows ₹2,499 and evidence supports ~₹1,499–1,699 India / $29 global. Owner: you, this week — nothing ships with a price in it until this closes.

5. **[FOUNDER — tone] How combative do we go?** The enemy angle names losers ("burst-posters, draft-queue babysitters, anyone paying a ghostwriter a client's worth of fees") — softened here versus the original draft's explicit "winners and losers" section, but still pointed. Ratify this register for founder-led DMs and posts, or soften further toward the customer-hero dignity framing.

6. **[FOUNDER — boldness of the "only" claim] "Only software that closes the loop on today's news, unattended."** This is the ICP's surviving claim #1, but Autoposting.ai is "Yes-capable, approval configurable" in the ICP's own table — a skeptic who googles finds it. The claim survives only with the *today's-news* qualifier attached; ratify that we never shorten it to "the only tool that auto-posts."

7. **[FOUNDER — math taste] The "1/35th" figure.** Kept in Pillar 2 (internal proof point) with the median cited; the customer-facing narrative uses raw numbers ($29 vs $1,000–$5,000) to avoid false-precision smell. Ratify this split, or drop the fraction entirely.

8. **[FOUNDER — strategy, not copy] The moat is one feature flag deep (ICP §6.7).** ViralBrain or Autoposting.ai can flip a news-feed flag in 1–2 quarters and "ghostwriter that posts" is one marketing pivot away for them. The durable wedge per the ICP is trust infrastructure (approval queue, provenance, dry-run) + owning the outcome metric (client conversations booked, not impressions). Commit build priority accordingly — the manifesto's Pillar 3 only stays true if the trust layer keeps its lead.

9. **[FOUNDER — telling prospects "no"] The anti-ICP line in customer-facing copy.** The narrative explicitly tells writing-lovers to keep their copilot. ICP §4 demands an anti-ICP, and saying it publicly buys credibility — but it does send some prospects away. Ratify saying it in the narrative itself (current draft) vs keeping it to sales conversations only.

10. **[FOUNDER — evidence base] Verbatim thinness.** The narrative's emotional spine now includes the pipeline-dry-spell quote in the body — the ICP's highest-intent trigger — but §6.5 warns all verbatims lean on Blind/Trustpilot/Substack, not this exact ICP. Run the first 10 customer interviews before locking exact phrasing; after them, swap the strongest *real customer* line into the opening.
